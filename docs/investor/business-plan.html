1. Executive Summary

ShowMePrice.ng is a trust-first Nigerian marketplace built for real prices, verified sellers, serious buyers, and safer high-value transactions.

Nigeria already has online marketplaces, classifieds, WhatsApp vendors, Instagram sellers, and social commerce. But the core problem remains unresolved: buyers and sellers still do not fully trust each other. Buyers fear fake listings, fake sellers, wrong products, advance-payment scams, and disappearing vendors. Sellers deal with unserious buyers, spam, fake payment claims, and endless "last price" conversations that never convert.

The thesis

Nigeria does not need another marketplace. Nigeria needs a marketplace where trust is built into the transaction flow — not bolted on as a verification badge.

The platform starts as a direct buyer-seller marketplace, but with stronger trust architecture than traditional classifieds: prices are visible upfront, sellers can be verified, buyers sign up with phone-first verification, free buyers communicate through in-app messaging, Pro buyers unlock direct WhatsApp/phone access, Premium buyers access escrow/protected transactions, high-risk transactions can be reviewed through admin dispute tools, and future phases expand into delivery, logistics partnerships, and fulfillment.

The market supports this thesis. Nigeria's e-commerce market is estimated at $9.35B in 2025, projected to reach $18.68B by 2031, with mobile commerce accounting for more than four-fifths of online orders. Social commerce is also expanding rapidly, projected at $2.04B in 2025 and $3.96B by 2030.

ShowMePrice's wedge is not to outspend incumbents like Jiji, Jumia, or Konga. The wedge is to own a narrower, higher-trust segment: high-intent Nigerian buyers and verified sellers trading products where price transparency, seller credibility, and payment safety matter.

2. The Problem

Nigeria's online buying behavior is already established. People buy through classifieds, WhatsApp, Instagram, Facebook, Jumia, Konga, and local vendors. The issue is not awareness of online commerce. The issue is trust after discovery.

A buyer may find a phone, laptop, generator, appliance, or car part online, but still has to ask:

Buyer concerns

  • Is this seller real?
  • Is this price real?
  • Is the product available?
  • Will the seller disappear after payment?
  • Should I pay before seeing the item?
  • If something goes wrong, who can help?

Seller concerns

  • Is this buyer serious or wasting time?
  • Will this "I'll send transfer" actually clear?
  • Is this an enquiry or a phishing attempt?
  • How do I prove I delivered if disputed?
  • Why am I getting 50 messages for 1 sale?

Traditional classifieds platforms maximize listing volume. Social commerce maximizes convenience. But neither fully solves the trust problem. Jiji has massive supply and category breadth, but like most classifieds models, trust depends heavily on user judgment, seller behavior, and off-platform communication. WhatsApp and Instagram commerce feel fast and familiar, but once negotiation moves off-platform, there is little structured evidence if a dispute happens.

ShowMePrice's position is different: the conversation, the price, the seller profile, the buyer status, the report trail, and the escrow record should live inside the marketplace. This is why in-app messaging is not just a feature — it is the trust enforcement layer.

3. The Insight

Most marketplaces treat trust as a badge. ShowMePrice treats trust as a system.

A verification badge alone does not prevent a seller from pushing a buyer to WhatsApp, asking for direct bank transfer, and disappearing. A buyer badge alone does not stop unserious buyers from wasting sellers' time. A report button alone does not protect a user in the moment of harassment or fraud.

ShowMePrice combines trust signals with trust controls:

The marketplace thesis follows: ShowMePrice is not just matching buyers and sellers. It is filtering for seriousness and preserving enough transaction context to reduce fraud and resolve disputes.

Nigerian online commerce is already large and still expanding.

The report identifies Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, and local e-commerce platforms as key parts of the competitive landscape.

This matters because ShowMePrice is not only competing with websites. It is competing with how Nigerians already buy: conversationally, socially, and mobile-first.

5. Target Customer

Primary buyer segment

ShowMePrice's first buyer segment is Nigerian buyers aged roughly 18–45 who are comfortable buying online but cautious about scams, fake sellers, fake prices, and payment risk.

Early focus categories should be high-intent and high-risk:

  • phones (especially used iPhones / Samsungs)
  • laptops
  • electronics
  • appliances
  • generators / power equipment
  • car parts (later)
  • business equipment (later)

These categories are ideal because buyers care about real price, seller credibility, product condition, location, negotiation, and payment safety — exactly the attributes ShowMePrice's trust architecture addresses.

Primary seller segment

The first seller segment is independent sellers, small shops, phone/laptop dealers, electronics sellers, appliance sellers, and local merchants who want serious buyers, not endless unserious inquiries.

ShowMePrice should recruit verified sellers first. The first 50–100 sellers matter more than thousands of random listings.

Future segments

Later, ShowMePrice can serve:

  • bulk buyers and institutional procurement
  • international and diaspora buyers
  • logistics partners and verified merchants needing fulfillment

But the first wedge remains focused: Lagos-first, high-risk categories, verified sellers, serious buyers.

7. Trust Architecture

ShowMePrice's trust architecture has five layers.

Layer 1: Price transparency

Every listing must show a price. This directly attacks "DM for price" behavior and makes ShowMePrice's name meaningful.

Layer 2: Verification

Buyer signup is phone-first via Termii. Seller verification begins manually (admin-reviewed identity documents) and graduates to automated NIN verification via Korapay Identity once activated.

Layer 3: Messaging trail

Free buyers use in-app messaging. This creates a platform-visible record for disputes, moderation, and trust scoring.

Layer 4: PII and payment-risk controls

Phase E ships a regex-based PII filter, seeded with 14 rules across 8 categories:

  • Hard block: WhatsApp / Telegram / Signal links, NUBAN bank accounts, payment URLs, shortened URLs, email addresses (free and Pro alike in listing descriptions)
  • Soft warning + allow: phone numbers, social handles (free buyers in chat)
  • Allow: WhatsApp links for Pro buyers in chat (part of the Pro value proposition)

ShowMePrice does not over-police normal Nigerian commerce — it protects against off-platform payment pressure and scam vectors specifically.

Layer 5: Escrow and dispute support

Escrow is reserved for high-value/protected transactions:

  • Escrow available for products priced ₦50,000+
  • Escrow fee = 1.5% of product price + ₦100 flat
  • Server-side recomputation required (fee never trusted from client)
  • Manual admin release/refund in Phase E
  • Buyer initiates escrow; seller must be active and verified/payout-ready

Escrow is a strong monetization and trust feature because it targets the exact transactions where fear is highest.

8. Business Model

ShowMePrice uses a multi-sided revenue model across six streams.

Stream 1: Pro Buyer subscription

Pro Buyer = serious buyer fast lane. Benefits include WhatsApp/phone contact reveal, direct call button, Pro Buyer badge shown to sellers, priority labeling in seller inboxes, SMS alerts for seller replies, Make an Offer, revealed contact history, higher conversation/offer limits, and better buyer profile surfacing.

Stream 2: Seller Premium (Phase F)

Seller monetization follows once buyer trust is working. Potential benefits: more active listings, verified badge priority, listing boosts, analytics, faster support, more media per listing, SMS notifications, better placement in trusted categories.

Stream 3: Escrow fee

1.5% + ₦100 flat per eligible protected transaction. This is a service fee for protection, dispute handling, and payment operations — not a take-rate on every transaction.

Stream 4: Promoted listings (Phase F)

Promoted listings come after trust quality is stable. Rule: do not let paid promotion override trust quality. A verified, responsive seller should rank better than a paying but low-quality seller.

Stream 5: Institution / bulk plans (Phase G)

Optional plans supporting bulk inquiries, custom buying requests, multiple buyer seats, invoice/procurement support, custom escrow terms, dedicated support. Must remain optional — ShowMePrice is still a direct buyer-seller marketplace; institutions should not force the product into RFQ/procurement-first behavior.

Stream 6: Logistics & fulfillment (Phase G+)

Future revenue from delivery coordination fees, logistics partner commissions, international buyer fulfillment, escrow + delivery bundles, and seller fulfillment services.

9. Go-To-Market Strategy

Phase 1: Lagos-first supply density

Start with Lagos — highest concentration of sellers, buyers, phone/laptop dealers, electronics shops, fast-moving commerce.

Goal: 50–100 verified sellers before aggressive buyer acquisition.

Phase 2: Trust-led buyer acquisition

Messaging focused on real prices, verified sellers, safer high-value buying, Pro buyer fast lane, escrow protection.

Find real prices. Message verified sellers. Use escrow for high-value purchases.

Phase 3: Category density

Do not launch every category equally. Start with: phones, laptops, electronics, appliances, generators/power equipment. These categories create the strongest case for trust and escrow.

Phase 4: Seller evangelism

Early verified sellers get: free Premium trial, verified badge, onboarding help, listing quality review, profile optimization, priority feedback channel. The first sellers become proof that ShowMePrice is serious.

Phase 5: Trust content

SEO + brand trust through content: how to avoid fake sellers, how escrow works, how to verify a seller, safe buying checklists by category (used iPhone, laptop, generator), why visible prices matter.

13. Defensibility & Moat

Marketplaces are easy to start and hard to defend. ShowMePrice's moat compounds with usage:

Trust data flywheel

Every in-app conversation, every escrow transaction, every dispute resolved produces structured data. After 12 months, ShowMePrice has reply-rate scorecards, dispute-rate-by-seller, escrow-failure profiles, and a PII-filter rule set tuned against real Nigerian scam patterns. None of this exists for new entrants from day zero.

Two-sided network effects

Verified sellers attract Pro buyers (who pay for contact + SMS). Pro buyers attract verified sellers (who get serious inquiries). Escrow adoption rises with seller verification rate. Each side reinforces the other — and the flywheel is gated on trust, not just listing count.

Operational moat

Dispute review, seller verification, escrow release/refund are human-operated in Phase E and human-supervised through Phase F. The team's playbook for "is this a scam pattern?" gets sharper each month. Cloning the product is easy; cloning two years of moderation data and decisions is not.

Brand positioning

ShowMePrice's name and positioning ("real prices, verified sellers") are unambiguous. Once buyers associate the brand with "I can trust this transaction," that association is hard to dislodge — especially after their first successful escrow purchase.

The moat is not the code or the schema. Both could be re-built. The moat is the accumulated trust data, the operational discipline, and the brand association — all of which take time to build and survive even significant feature gaps versus competitors.

16. Risk Analysis

Risk 1: Marketplace liquidity

Risk: If there are not enough quality listings, buyers leave. Mitigation: Lagos-first, category focus, recruit verified sellers before launching buyer acquisition, avoid premature category expansion.

Risk 2: Trust failure

Risk: One major scam story can damage the brand. Mitigation: Seller verification at signup, in-app messaging by default, payment-risk blocking via PII filter, escrow for high-value items, manual dispute review with documented response times.

Risk 3: Too much friction

Risk: If ShowMePrice feels harder than WhatsApp or Jiji, users bounce. Mitigation: Phone-first signup is one screen, free in-app messaging matches WhatsApp UX, Pro unlock for direct contact respects existing behavior, soft warnings instead of hard-blocking phone numbers in chat.

Risk 4: Escrow operational burden

Risk: Escrow creates responsibility — disputes consume operational hours. Mitigation: Manual release/refund first (no automation), server-side fee recomputation, clear admin logs, 1–2 trained operators dedicated to escrow review.

Risk 5: Competing with incumbents

Risk: Jiji has scale, Jumia has brand, WhatsApp has habit. Mitigation: Do not compete broadly. Own trust-first direct trade. Focus high-risk categories. Build seller/buyer trust loops first, scale second.

Risk 6: Vendor dependency

Risk: Termii / Korapay / Paystack approval delays or outages. Mitigation: Vendor-agnostic interfaces shipped at Stage 1 (Phase E.1.7). PaymentGateway + NinVerifier interfaces support swapping vendors without app-code rewrites. Documented fallback paths: SecureOTP for Termii, Phase C.5 manual verification for Korapay, KorapayGateway as documented payment fallback.

Risk 7: Regulatory exposure

Risk: Nigerian Data Protection Regulation (NDPR), CBN payment-services oversight, e-commerce consumer protection. Mitigation: NIN data handled via licensed providers (Korapay), payment processing via licensed processors (Paystack), CAC registration current, founder-managed compliance posture with reserve in funding plan for legal review of escrow Terms of Service.

20. Founder Q&A

Why will this win when Jiji already exists?

Jiji proves the behavior exists. Nigerians already buy and sell through classifieds. But the trust layer is still weak. We're not trying to beat Jiji by having more listings on day one. We're starting with categories where trust matters most — phones, laptops, electronics, appliances — and building a marketplace where price transparency, verified sellers, serious-buyer signals, in-app messaging, and escrow are part of the transaction flow. The next opportunity isn't more listings. It's safer, more serious trade.

Why now?

Nigerian e-commerce and social commerce are both growing, but trust remains the bottleneck. Buyers are already online. Sellers are already online. Payments are improving. Logistics is improving. But people still hesitate when the transaction is high-value. ShowMePrice enters at that trust gap.

What's your wedge?

Serious buyers and verified sellers in high-risk Nigerian product categories. Lagos-first. We are not starting with everything — we're starting where trust and price transparency matter most.

Why solo founder?

Phase E Stage 1 needed deep technical execution — schema design, RLS hardening, vendor abstractions, deployment infrastructure. Solo execution kept the architecture coherent and capital-efficient. The next phase needs operators, not engineers: trust & safety, seller growth, and customer support are the first three hires post-funding. Engineering capacity returns in Phase F when the operational base is wide enough to need it.

What if Termii / Korapay don't approve?

Phase E.1.7 ships vendor-agnostic interfaces for both payments (PaymentGateway) and identity (NinVerifier). PaymentGateway has a documented Korapay fallback target. NIN verification has a documented Phase C.5 manual-verification fallback. The application code never imports a vendor SDK directly — vendor swaps are config changes, not rewrites. The risk is timeline, not architecture.

What's the exit story?

Strategic acquisition by a Nigerian fintech / payments company (Paystack, Flutterwave, Interswitch) or a pan-African marketplace consolidator at Series B. The trust infrastructure + escrow data + verified-seller pipeline is more valuable to an existing payments or commerce player than the listing graph alone. Alternative: continued growth into a profitable, independent marketplace business — base case Year 3 economics support this without further dilution.
